Eiffel Tower (Tour Eiffel)

Source: Wikipedia, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eiffel_Tower

Overview
--------
The Eiffel Tower is a wrought-iron lattice tower on the Champ de Mars in Paris, France.
It is named after the engineer Gustave Eiffel, whose company designed and built the tower
from 1887 to 1889. Locally nicknamed "La dame de fer" (French for "Iron Lady"), it was
constructed as the centrepiece of the 1889 World's Fair and to crown the centennial
anniversary of the French Revolution. Although initially criticised by some of France's
leading artists and intellectuals for its design, it has since become a global cultural
icon of France and one of the most recognisable structures in the world.

Key Facts
---------
- Type: Observation tower, broadcasting tower
- Location: 7th arrondissement of Paris, France
- Coordinates: 48.8584° N, 2.2945° E
- Construction started: 28 January 1887
- Completed: 31 March 1889
- Opened to the public: 15 May 1889
- Owner: City of Paris, France
- Operator: Société d'Exploitation de la Tour Eiffel (SETE)
- Architectural height: 300 m (984 ft)
- Height to tip (with antenna): 330 m (1,083 ft)
- Height of top floor: 276 m (906 ft)
- Floor count: 4 (3 accessible to visitors)
- Lifts/elevators: 8
- Base: square, 125 m (410 ft) on each side
- Mass: approximately 7,300 tonnes of wrought iron (plus ~60 tonnes of paint)
- Designated a monument historique in 1964
- Part of UNESCO World Heritage Site "Paris, Banks of the Seine" since 1991
- Was the tallest human-made structure in the world from 1889 until 1930
  (41 years), when it was surpassed by the Chrysler Building in New York City.
- First structure in the world to exceed both the 200 m and 300 m height marks.

Designers and Construction
--------------------------
- Original design concept: Maurice Koechlin and Émile Nouguier, two senior engineers
  working for the Compagnie des Établissements Eiffel. Koechlin made the first sketch
  in May 1884, describing it as "a great pylon, consisting of four lattice girders
  standing apart at the base and coming together at the top, joined together by metal
  trusses at regular intervals".
- Architectural refinements: Stephen Sauvestre, head of the company's architectural
  department (added decorative arches at the base, a glass pavilion on the first level,
  and other embellishments).
- Structural engineers: Maurice Koechlin and Émile Nouguier.
- Architect of record: Stephen Sauvestre.
- Main contractor: Compagnie des Établissements Eiffel (owned by Gustave Eiffel).
- Construction contract signed: 8 January 1887. Eiffel signed in his own capacity and
  was granted 1.5 million francs toward construction (less than a quarter of the
  estimated 6.5 million franc cost). He was to receive all commercial income from the
  tower for 20 years.
- Financing: partly funded through loans from the Crédit Industriel et Commercial (CIC)
  bank, which (controversially) included predatory loans to the National Bank of Haiti
  connected to the Haiti indemnity controversy.

History
-------
- Origin: Designed as the centrepiece of the 1889 Exposition Universelle, celebrating
  the centennial of the French Revolution. Koechlin's initial 1884 sketch evolved
  through Sauvestre's architectural input. Eiffel's plans were presented to the
  Société des Ingénieurs Civils on 30 March 1885. The project was effectively locked
  in on 1 May 1886 when the terms of the open competition were altered to require a
  300 m four-sided metal tower on the Champ de Mars.
- Artists' protest (1887): A "Committee of Three Hundred" (one member per metre of
  height), led by architect Charles Garnier and including artists such as
  William-Adolphe Bouguereau, Guy de Maupassant, Charles Gounod, and Jules Massenet,
  published a petition called "Artists against the Eiffel Tower" in Le Temps on
  14 February 1887. The petition called the tower "useless and monstrous" and warned
  it would dominate Paris "like a gigantic black smokestack". Eiffel countered by
  comparing the tower to the Egyptian pyramids: "My tower will be the tallest edifice
  ever erected by man. Will it not also be grandiose in its way? And why would
  something admirable in Egypt become hideous and ridiculous in Paris?" Maupassant is
  said to have dined daily in the tower's restaurant because it was the only place in
  Paris where the tower was not visible.
- Construction: Work on the foundations began 28 January 1887. The east and south legs
  rested on four 2 m concrete slabs each. The west and north legs, closer to the Seine,
  required compressed-air caissons 15 m long and 6 m in diameter driven to 22 m depth
  to support 6 m thick concrete slabs, which in turn supported limestone blocks with
  inclined tops to bear the ironwork.
- Inauguration: Opened on 15 May 1889 as the entrance arch to the 1889 World's Fair.
- Subsequent events: Used as a giant radio antenna; a permanent radio aerial added at
  the top in 1957 made it 11 m taller than the Chrysler Building. Survived attempts to
  dismantle and sell it for scrap after its 20-year permit expired in 1909. Played
  important roles in both World Wars, including as a radio transmission tower. Has
  been painted approximately every 7 years to prevent rust; the current paint scheme
  since 1968 uses three shades (darker at the bottom, lighter at the top) to enhance
  the visual impression of uniformity with the sky.

Design
------
- Material: Wrought iron (puddled iron, produced in Pompey, Meurthe-et-Moselle, by
  the Pompey steelworks). About 7,300 tonnes of iron, 2.5 million rivets, and 18,038
  individual iron pieces. The iron was chosen for its strength, light weight, and
  resistance to corrosion.
- Wind resistance: Designed to resist wind forces; the structure only sways a few
  centimetres at the top even in strong winds. Wind considerations significantly
  influenced the parabolic curvature of the legs.
- Floors:
  - Ground floor: open-air base, accessible for free; houses exhibits and a small
    souvenir shop.
  - 1st floor (57 m / 187 ft): glass-walled pavilion (designed by Sauvestre); features
    the 58 Tour Eiffel restaurant (renovated in 2022) and the Macaron bar by
    Pierre Hermé. Has transparent floor sections installed in 2014.
  - 2nd floor (115 m / 377 ft): features the Jules Verne restaurant (one-Michelin-star
    as of recent years) and an observation area.
  - 3rd floor / top (276 m / 906 ft): the highest public observation deck in the
    European Union. Includes a private apartment originally built for Gustave Eiffel,
    furnished with furniture by Jean Lachaise. Hosts Thomas Edison's recorded message
    and wax figures of Eiffel and Edison.
- Lifts: 8 lifts total, including the original hydraulic lifts designed by the Otis
  Elevator Company. Original lifts travelled at a modest speed; modern replacements
  have updated the system while preserving the appearance of the original machinery
  rooms on view at the base.
- Stairs: 1,665 steps from ground to top. Visitors may walk or climb stairs to the
  first and second levels. The climb from ground to first level is over 300 steps, as
  is the climb from the first to the second level (total 600 steps). The top level is
  only reachable by lift.
- Engraved names: On the tower just above the first level, the names of 72 prominent
  French scientists, engineers, and mathematicians are engraved in 60 cm letters
  (later painted over but still visible), in recognition of their contributions.
- Aesthetics: Eiffel hired French architect Stephen Sauvestre to add ornamental
  touches that helped win public approval. The graceful parabolic curve of the legs
  is the result of engineering calculations, not arbitrary style.
- Maintenance: Repainted approximately every 7 years. The full repaint cycle requires
  about 60 tonnes of paint. Until 2010 the tower was painted by hand; the work
  transitioned to specialised rollers. The 2014–2025 repaint cycle was the first to
  use a paint free of lead (which had been standard until then), and the first to
  introduce an anti-graffiti coating.

Communications
--------------
- Used as a radio transmission tower from early in its existence. A 1920s antenna
  atop the tower made it the tallest structure in the world again for a time.
- Carries numerous FM radio and digital television transmitters serving the Paris
  region.
- Notable analog TV transmitter serving France was shut down with the digital
  transition.

Dimensions and Height
---------------------
- The tower's height varies by a few centimetres depending on temperature, since
  the iron expands when heated. On cold days the tower can be up to 15 cm shorter
  than on hot days.
- The 330 m height to tip includes the current top antenna (installed 1957, replaced
  several times since). Without the antenna, the architectural height remains 300 m.
- Excluding transmitters, the Eiffel Tower is the second tallest free-standing
  structure in France after the Millau Viaduct.

Tourism
-------
- Most visited paid monument in the world: 6.91 million visitors in 2015; 5.89
  million in 2022 (lower post-COVID).
- Average of about 7 million visitors per year in normal times.
- Transport access: Closest Métro stations are Bir-Hakeim and École Militaire
  (line 6); RER C station Champ de Mars – Tour Eiffel. Numerous bus lines and the
  Batobus river shuttle also serve the site.
- Stair access tickets are cheaper than lift access, but more strenuous.

Cultural Significance and Trivia
--------------------------------
- Has become the global symbol of Paris and of France.
- Featured prominently in countless films, books, and works of art.
- An illumination copyright (described as "luminous heritage") protects the specific
  sparkling-light display installed by Pierre Bideau in 1985; this is separate from
  the tower's image copyright.
- Sparkles for 5 minutes every hour after dark, using ~20,000 bulbs.
- Has hosted sporting events (climbing races, cycling), BASE jumping stunts, and
  a temporary ice rink.
- Used in scientific experiments multiple times, including Einstein's
  involvement in radio contact with the United States in 1924, and as a platform
  for early radio and television broadcasting experiments.

Replicas
--------
- Numerous full-size and scaled replicas exist worldwide. Notable ones include:
  - Tokyo Tower (Japan, 1958) — 333 m, inspired by but taller than the Eiffel Tower
  - Las Vegas replica (Paris Las Vegas hotel) at half scale
  - Replicas in Shenzhen (China), Slobozia (Romania), and many US cities
  - A replica at the Window of the World theme park in Shenzhen
  - A replica at Paris Las Vegas (half scale, 165 m)
  - The Blackpool Tower (UK, 1894) and the 1:3 replica at the Pilton Park "Eiffel
    Tower" in Paris, Texas are other notable examples.
